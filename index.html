Know Before You Decide

Robotic or Traditional —
Which Do You Actually Need?

Robotic assistance makes an experienced doctor better.
It's the experience that counts, not just the robot.

Dr. Allahbadia has performed over 10,000 successful knee surgeries — the overwhelming majority without robotic assistance.

This is not a page selling you on a robot. It is an honest explanation so you can make the right decision for yourself.

In a traditional knee replacement, the surgeon uses hand instruments and metal alignment rods to make bone cuts and position the implant. The result depends on the surgeon's skill, experience, and feel — and when those are excellent, the outcomes are excellent.

In a robotic-assisted replacement like CORI, the surgeon performs every step — but the system builds a live 3D map of your knee during surgery and guides the cutting tool with sub-millimetre accuracy. The robot does not operate. It assists. Think of it as the difference between a skilled craftsman and a skilled craftsman with precision instruments.

No — and he will tell you this plainly. Dr. Allahbadia performed over 10,000 successful knee surgeries before CORI technology was available in India. Traditional surgery, in the right hands, produces outstanding results. Many of his patients have been walking pain-free for 15–20 years after a conventional knee replacement.

His view: the robot makes a good surgeon better. It does not make a poor surgeon good. If a surgeon lacks clinical judgement, robotic assistance will not compensate for that. The tool is only as valuable as the hand holding it.

Robotic precision adds the most value where small alignment errors carry big consequences — younger patients wanting an implant that outlasts their working life, severe deformities, revision surgeries (CORI is the only robot approved for revision TKR), and high-BMI patients where accurate cuts are technically harder.

For a standard, well-aligned knee in an older patient without unusual anatomy, the difference in outcome between expert traditional and expert robotic surgery is often small. Dr. Allahbadia will tell you honestly which category you fall into.

Robotic surgery's precise cuts mean less soft tissue trauma — and often less post-operative pain. Most CORI patients walk the day after surgery and go home within 48 to 72 hours.

Recovery from a well-performed conventional surgery is also excellent — walking the next day, back to daily life in 2–3 weeks. Regardless of technique, physiotherapy is non-negotiable. It is the single biggest determinant of your long-term outcome.

A robot in the hands of a surgeon with limited experience or poor judgement will not produce a good outcome. The system guides — it does not think, plan, or compensate for inadequate surgical decision-making.

When choosing where to have your knee replaced, ask first: how many has this surgeon done? What is their revision rate? How do their patients fare at 5, 10, 15 years? Those answers matter far more than whether a robot was in the room.
